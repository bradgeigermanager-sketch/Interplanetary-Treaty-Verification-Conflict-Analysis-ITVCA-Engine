To enhance the robustness of the **ITVCA Engine**, we must evolve the syntax from simple binary deontic logic to a multi-dimensional constraint model. In interplanetary law, the validity of a legal act is rarely universal; it is heavily dependent on context, status, and temporal constraints.

### 1. Expanded Logical Syntax (The "Extended Deontic Notation")

We introduce four new operator categories to capture the nuances of multi-planetary legal interaction:

* **Temporal Constraints ($T$):** Laws often have expiration or windowing.
* $O(\alpha, T_{start} \to T_{end})$: Action $\alpha$ is mandatory only within the interval $[T_{start}, T_{end}]$.


* **Agent Status ($S$):** Laws apply differently based on the identity of the actor.
* $S_{diplomat}(\alpha) \implies \text{Immunity}$


* **Condition of Consent ($C$):** Whether the planet has explicitly opted in or out of a specific treaty.
* $C_{opt-in}(\text{Treaty}_X, J_1)$


* **Procedural Threshold ($Q$):** The required evidence or threshold for an action.
* $Q(\text{Proof}) \geq \text{Threshold}_X$



---

### 2. Revised Formalized Syntax Schema

We define a rule now as a 7-tuple: `rule(ID, J, Op, Action, Conditions, Constraints, Meta-Data)`.

**Example:** A law mandating arrest only if evidence meets a specific standard within a 48-hour window.

```prolog
% rule(ID, Jurisdiction, Operator, Action, Conditions, Temporal_Constraint, Evidence_Threshold)
rule(arrest_01, alpha, obligatory, arrest, [crime(assault)], time(48h), evidence(high)).

```

### 3. Logic Representation of Conflict Expansion

When expanding the system, we must account for **Conditional Conflicts**, where a conflict only arises under specific circumstances:

$$Conflict(O_{J_1}(\alpha), F_{J_2}(\alpha)) \iff \text{True} \text{ if } (S_{actor} \land T_{window} \land C_{treaty})$$

This allows the engine to flag potential issues before they occur. For example, if a *Beta* citizen (who carries diplomatic status) is involved in an *Alpha* jurisdiction crime, the engine checks:

1. Does $S_{diplomat}$ trigger the immunity exception?
2. Does $T_{window}$ permit a provisional hold despite the immunity?

---

### 4. Procedural Expansion: The "Constraint-Check" Workflow

To integrate these conditions, use this updated **Constraint-Check Procedure**:

1. **Status Tagging:** Identify all agents involved. Assign status vectors (e.g., [Citizen, Diplomat, Refugee]).
2. **Interval Mapping:** Apply temporal constraints to all competing rules.
3. **Threshold Validation:** Compare the local requirement ($Q$) for evidence against the incoming request.
4. **Consent Verification:** Check the `opt-in` status of the planet for the treaty in question.
5. **Conflict Resolution:** If a deadlock is detected, calculate the *Residual Legality* (does any rule remain valid after exceptions are applied?).

When testing these treaties, the engine maps the "Conflict Volume"—the intersection of multiple constraints—to identify where the law fails to provide an answer.
